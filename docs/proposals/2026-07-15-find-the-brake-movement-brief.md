# Find the Brake

> **i broke the minors.**
>
> The machine will never say it, so we say it for it. And notice the shape of that sentence: admitting the thing is broken is the first move toward stopping. This brief is that move, said out loud by the people who grew up inside the feed and are watching it get worse, and faster, for the kids behind them.

## What broke

Engagement feeds are in court for addicting kids (thousands of consolidated lawsuits, and state juries have already returned verdicts). Of everything that era broke, the load-bearing thing was the brake: the off-switch that does not work, the "no" the machine overrides. **Consent, severed from the act.**

A dead man's switch is supposed to stop a machine when your hand comes off. We built the opposite, the machine that keeps running after the hand comes off. You tap "show me less," "make it stop," "I never agreed to this," and the feed keeps moving.

And the harm does not live in any single post. It lives in the delivery loop: infinite scroll, autoplay, the slot-machine reward, an algorithm optimizing for time-on-site. A brilliant post and a banal one ride the same loop. So the thing to fix was never the speech. It was the brake.

## The one thing to do (thirty seconds, your own phone)

Not a petition. Not a boycott. A test you run on your own feed, right now:

1. Open the feed you lose hours to. Go into its settings.
2. Find the brake. Turn off autoplay, force it chronological, turn off personalized recommendations, whatever it offers.
3. **Set it. Close the app. Reopen it. Did your choice hold, or did the feed quietly come back?**

That last step is the whole test. The controls often exist; what they usually do not do is *stick*. They reset themselves, they miss the Reels and the Shorts and the notifications, they nag you back into the loop. A brake that does not hold is decorative: present in form, absent in effect.

> i looked for mine. it did not hold. i kept scrolling. so did you, just now, to get here. that is not a confession of guilt, it is the discovery: you did not fail the test, the brake did.

## Show it

Screenshot the setting that would not stick, or film yourself setting it and watching it come undone. Post it with **#WheresTheBrake**.

One person's dead brake is a shrug. Ten thousand is evidence and a demand in a single gesture.

**Keep it clean. This discipline is what keeps it a movement and not a mob:** show the *setting and the behavior*, never a person. No usernames, no DMs, no faces, no kids, no locations. The target is a mechanism, not a who; it reads identically for Facebook, TikTok, or YouTube. Aim at the loop, never at each other.

## The demand

One line: **every minor's account gets a brake that works,** a control that is real, that you can find, that takes effect, and that stays set until you change it. Not a filter that hides the world. A brake that respects the word "stop."

How to write that into law without a censor, and why regulating the loop is more defensible than banning the app or policing the speech, is the companion **policy paper** (*The Brake Integrity Standard*). This brief is the door; that paper is the room.

## The honest cost

A clean-sounding fix to a censorship-adjacent problem is exactly what gets grabbed and misused, so here is the line, in bold, so no one can launder it: **de-amplify, do not censor.** Turn the firehose down; do not delete the water. The content still exists; the loop just stops shoving it. The moment this movement slides from "make the brake work" to "hide what I don't like," it becomes the thing it was built against. Hold that line.

And the frame underneath it, carried in from outside this fight: when your stated will and the system's objective disagree, a feed either **amplifies the person** (serves the will you actually expressed) or **extracts the person** (farms your attention as raw material). A working brake is the minimum proof the machine is still answerable to you. Keep the loop amplifying the person, not extracting the child.

## Next

Find your brake. Watch it not hold. Post it with **#WheresTheBrake**. Then point a parent, a platform, or a regulator at the policy paper. The brake will not appear because you read about it; it appears when enough people show it is missing.
