## Reader summary

This is the big federal case bundling thousands of lawsuits that accuse Meta (Facebook, Instagram), Google/Alphabet (YouTube), ByteDance (TikTok), and Snap (Snapchat) of building their apps to be addictive to kids and teens. All of those cases are being managed together in one federal court in Northern California, in front of U.S. District Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers, under the label "MDL 3047." As of July 1, 2026, 2,893 individual lawsuits were pending in that bundle. On June 30, 2026, the judge refused to throw out the claims brought by a group of state attorneys general, so those claims are headed to trial. A first trial starts in August 2026 in Oakland: it combines federal children's-privacy (COPPA) claims from 29 states with consumer-protection claims from four of those states (California, Colorado, Kentucky, and New Jersey). Meta has said those four states are seeking penalties that could theoretically add up to about $1.4 trillion — a number Meta says is wildly overstated. Nothing here means Meta or the other companies have been found liable yet; the trial will decide that. (Everything below is current as of July 16, 2026, and litigation like this changes almost weekly.)

Note: This document covers ONLY the federal case, MDL 3047. Separate state-court cases (including a California state proceeding and a New Mexico case, both of which produced their own jury verdicts) are deliberately kept out to avoid mixing up the facts.

## Claims (the ledger)

### (a) Parties & court

1. **[ESTABLISHED]** The case is formally titled *In re: Social Media Adolescent Addiction/Personal Injury Products Liability Litigation*, MDL No. 3047, and appears on the federal Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation (JPML) docket list. — *Source: JPML Pending MDL Dockets report, July 1, 2026, page 1.*

2. **[ESTABLISHED]** The MDL is assigned to U.S. District Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers in the Northern District of California (district code "CAN"). — *Source: JPML report, July 1, 2026, page 1; corroborated by California AG release and Top Class Actions.*

3. **[ESTABLISHED]** As of the July 1, 2026 JPML report, MDL 3047 had **2,893** actions "now pending," with a historical total of 3,068 actions ever filed into the docket. — *Source: JPML report, July 1, 2026, page 1 (line: "Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers … MDL-3047 … 2,893 … 3,068").*

4. **[OBSERVED]** The lead consolidated case carries the individual number 4:23-cv-05448-YGR (the MDL number is docketed as 22-md-03047). — *Source: Top Class Actions.*

5. **[OBSERVED]** The named corporate defendants are Meta (Facebook/Instagram), Google/Alphabet (YouTube), ByteDance (TikTok), and Snap (Snapchat). Secondary coverage of the settled bellwether names the defendant set as "Meta, Snap, TikTok, and YouTube." — *Source: verified baseline; defendant set corroborated by Law Commentary and web-search coverage of the Kentucky bellwether settlement.*

6. **[OBSERVED]** The state attorneys general are litigating as a coalition; secondary coverage puts the group bringing federal children's-privacy (COPPA) claims at **29 states**, including California, New Jersey, Kentucky, and Colorado. (The official California AG release refers to "attorneys general nationwide" but does not state a number.) — *Source: Top Class Actions; JURIST; Engadget; California AG release (no count).* See open question in Tensions about how the "29" and the "four" states relate.

### (b) The legal theory

7. **[ESTABLISHED / OBSERVED]** The core theory is **product design**: plaintiffs allege the companies intentionally built app features to keep young users coming back. Court coverage lists the specific features at issue as "infinite scroll, autoplay, notifications and other engagement-focused tools." — *Source: verified baseline (theory); the feature list is quoted in Top Class Actions' account of the order.*

8. **[OBSERVED]** A central factual fight is whether "social media addiction" is a real, definable condition. The judge found that "more recent research and statements from the American Psychiatric Association create a factual dispute regarding the existence and characteristics of social media addiction that must be resolved at trial." Meta argues social-media addiction is not an established psychiatric diagnosis; the American Psychiatric Association's position is that the absence of a DSM-5-TR listing does not mean the condition does not exist. — *Source: Top Class Actions (quoting the order); Engadget.*

### (c) The June 30, 2026 ruling

9. **[ESTABLISHED]** On June 30, 2026, the court **denied Meta's motion for summary judgment** as to the state attorneys general's claims — meaning those claims survive and proceed toward trial rather than being thrown out. The California AG describes the motion as "fully denied." — *Source: California AG press release; corroborated by Top Class Actions.*

10. **[ESTABLISHED / OBSERVED]** The denial rested on **material factual disputes** — including whether the platforms are addictive and whether Meta misrepresented that — which a court cannot resolve on summary judgment and must send to trial. — *Source: verified baseline; supported by the "factual dispute … must be resolved at trial" language in Top Class Actions' account of the order.*

11. **[ESTABLISHED]** On one narrower COPPA point, the court gave the **states a partial win**: it found there is "no factual dispute that Meta did not comply with the statute's notice and parental consent requirements." The California AG frames this as the court agreeing that Meta "did not obtain parental consent in a manner sufficient to satisfy" COPPA. — *Source: California AG press release; Top Class Actions (quoting the order).*

12. **[ESTABLISHED / OBSERVED]** This COPPA point is a **compliance finding, not a finding of liability.** Whether COPPA even applies to the services (questions like child-directed status and the company's knowledge of under-13 users) remains disputed and is left for trial: "factual disputes remain regarding whether COPPA applies to Meta, leaving that liability question for trial." — *Source: Top Class Actions (quoting the order); framing consistent with verified baseline.*

13. **[OBSERVED — baseline / Law.com, NOT independently verified]** Per legal-press accounts of the same order, the court found that Meta's own documents could support the plaintiffs' theory that Meta's time-restriction (screen-time-limit) tools were a "public relations stunt" — deployed while the company allegedly knew more time on the platform was linked to worse outcomes for teens. This is **cited evidence that could support the theory, still disputed — not a finding of fact.** — *Source: verified baseline, attributed to Law.com/The Recorder coverage of the order. The Law.com article returned HTTP 403 and could not be fetched; the "public relations stunt" phrase did not surface in any source this document was able to access. Flagged for human verification.*

### (d) The August trial + follow-on

14. **[ESTABLISHED]** The first trial is set for **August 2026 in Oakland, California**. **Jury selection begins August 12, 2026**, and **opening statements begin August 18, 2026.** — *Source: California AG press release.*

15. **[OBSERVED]** The August trial combines two sets of claims: the **29 states' consolidated federal COPPA claims** and **consumer-protection claims from four states — California, Colorado, Kentucky, and New Jersey.** — *Source: JURIST; Engadget; Law Commentary; Top Class Actions.*

16. **[OBSERVED]** The trial is structured to use an **advisory jury**. Meta had demanded a jury, then **withdrew that demand**; the judge agreed to the withdrawal but said that in a bench trial she would likely seat an advisory jury to hear the evidence and give its view — while **she retains the ultimate findings.** She is expected to decide the federal COPPA claims herself and use the advisory jury chiefly on the state consumer-protection claims. — *Source: Law360 headline and MLex, surfaced via web search (not directly fetched); consistent with verified baseline.*

17. **[OBSERVED]** A **second trial for 14 additional states**, trying their own state-law claims, is scheduled for **February 2027.** — *Source: Engadget; consistent with verified baseline.*

18. **[OBSERVED]** Counting the tracks together, more than 40 state attorneys general have brought claims in the overall litigation (29 COPPA states + 14 February-2027 states ≈ the "more than 40" figure). — *Source: JURIST ("more than 40 state AGs"); arithmetic from Engadget's 29 + 14.*

### (e) Money / exposure

19. **[OBSERVED]** In its own court filing, Meta characterized the **four states' potential penalty exposure as up to about $1.4 trillion.** — *Source: JURIST; Engadget; Law Commentary; consistent with verified baseline.*

20. **[OBSERVED]** The $1.4 trillion figure comes from **multiplying a very large count of qualifying young users by the maximum per-violation statutory penalty** under each applicable law. One account describes the states' method as "counting every teenage user of a Meta platform and then multiplying that count by every month in which a teen spent more than 30 minutes." — *Source: JURIST (user count × maximum fine per law); Law Commentary (per-month calculation detail).*

21. **[OBSERVED]** Meta argues the figure is improper and enormous, calling the demand "extraordinary and unjustified" and saying "a sanction of that size has no analog in the history of consumer protection enforcement." Meta also contends each state-law penalty must be tied to a "separate, affirmative act" of wrongdoing and raises due-process concerns. — *Source: Engadget and Law Commentary (Meta's quoted language); JURIST (the "separate, affirmative act" and due-process arguments).* See Discrepancies for the wording "unmoored" vs "unjustified."

22. **[ASSUMED]** The $1.4 trillion figure is a **theoretical statutory ceiling** — the largest number arithmetically possible under the penalty formula — not a demand any court has awarded or is likely to award in full. — *This is the drafter's characterization of how statutory-maximum penalty figures work, offered for reader context; the exact number the states will actually seek at trial, and any amount a court might award, are not established in the record reviewed here.*

### (f) Settlements

23. **[OBSERVED]** All four defendants **settled the first federal bellwether case** (a personal-injury / product-liability test case — reported to be a Kentucky school-district plaintiff) **before it reached a jury.** The settlement was reported at **near $27 million combined**, with formal terms undisclosed; one account attributes Meta's portion as about **$9 million** and dates the settlement to May 2026. — *Source: verified baseline; Law Commentary (Kentucky school-district defendants settling); JURIST ($27M total, Meta's portion $9M, May).* Note the tension between "terms formally undisclosed" and the specific dollar figures in secondary reporting (see Tensions).

24. **[ESTABLISHED / OBSERVED]** The bellwether settlement involved **no admission of liability** and produced **no publicly disclosed, enforceable design standard.** — *Source: verified baseline; consistent with the "terms undisclosed" secondary reporting.*

### (g) Current status (as of 2026-07-16)

25. **[ESTABLISHED / OBSERVED]** The state attorneys general's claims survived summary judgment and are **headed to trial**; the first trial (29-state COPPA + 4-state consumer protection) begins with jury selection August 12, 2026, opening statements August 18, 2026, in Oakland. — *Source: California AG release (dates); Top Class Actions (survival of claims).*

26. **[OBSERVED]** No defendant has been found liable in MDL 3047 as of this document's date; liability on the surviving claims remains to be decided at the August trial and the February 2027 trial. — *Source: reasonable synthesis of the June 30 ruling (denial of summary judgment leaves liability for trial) and the compliance-not-liability nature of the COPPA finding.*

## Tensions / open questions

- **Are the "four states" a subset of the "29 states," or additional to them?** The baseline and Top Class Actions treat the four consumer-protection states (CA, CO, KY, NJ) as being *among* the 29 COPPA states — i.e., those four bring both COPPA claims and extra consumer-protection claims. Engadget's phrasing ("Four states… 29 additional states") could be read as 4 + 29 = 33 separate states. The more careful reading (four are within the 29) is better supported, but the exact overlap should be confirmed against the court's own trial-plan order.
- **"Terms formally undisclosed" vs. specific settlement dollars.** The baseline says the bellwether settlement terms were formally undisclosed, yet secondary coverage reports a combined figure near $27 million (and a ~$9 million Meta portion). These can both be true (unofficial reporting of an officially sealed deal), but the dollar amounts should be treated as reported estimates, not confirmed terms.
- **"Bellwether" is used for two different things.** The *settled* bellwether was a personal-injury/product-liability test case (a school-district plaintiff). The *August* trial is the state attorneys general's trial. Both get called "bellwether" in coverage; they are different tracks within the same MDL and should not be conflated.
- **The "$1.4 trillion" is a ceiling, not a verdict.** It is Meta's own framing of the maximum arithmetic exposure. What the states will actually argue for, and what a court could award, are unresolved.

## Discrepancies vs the verified baseline

- **Meta's characterization of the $1.4T figure — "unmoored" (baseline) vs. "unjustified" (fetched sources).** The baseline says Meta calls the figure "unmoored." The accessible sources this document reviewed quote Meta as calling the demand "extraordinary and unjustified" (Law Commentary), "unjustified" with "no analog in the history of consumer protection enforcement" (Engadget), and "improper" (JURIST). None of the fetched sources used the exact word "unmoored." Both are recorded here for human adjudication; the baseline word "unmoored" could not be confirmed against an accessible source.
- No other direct factual conflicts were found. The verified baseline's load-bearing facts (2,893 pending actions; Judge Gonzalez Rogers; N.D. Cal.; June 30 denial of summary judgment; partial COPPA compliance finding; Aug 12 jury selection / Aug 18 opening statements; four states = CA/CO/KY/NJ; 29 COPPA states; 14 states in Feb 2027; ~$1.4T exposure; ~$27M bellwether settlement) were each corroborated by at least one fetched source, except the items noted under "Coverage note."
