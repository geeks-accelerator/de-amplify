## Reader summary

As of July 16, 2026, there is a large group of lawsuits in California state court — not the separate federal case — that accuse social media companies of designing their apps to be addictive and harming young users. About 1,600 of these suits have been bundled together in a single coordinated proceeding called JCCP 5255, run out of Los Angeles Superior Court. A handful of "bellwether" (test) cases are being tried first to show how juries might react. In the first bellwether, K.G.M. v. Meta and Google, a jury returned a $6 million verdict on March 25, 2026 against Meta (which owns Instagram) and Google (which owns YouTube). On June 10, 2026, the trial judge turned down the companies' requests to throw out or re-run the case, and both companies have since filed notices that they will appeal. Important: that verdict is being appealed — it has NOT been reviewed or approved by any appeals court yet. A second test case is set for trial on July 27, 2026, but only after YouTube and TikTok quietly settled their part of it, leaving Meta and Snap to face the jury.

## Claims (the ledger)

### (a) The JCCP 5255 proceeding and the court

1. **[ESTABLISHED]** The California cases are consolidated in a state-court coordinated proceeding known as JCCP 5255 (a Judicial Council Coordination Proceeding). *(Verified baseline; corroborated by web search summaries of the case coverage.)*

2. **[ESTABLISHED]** This proceeding is separate from the federal multidistrict litigation (MDL 3047). JCCP 5255 is a California *state*-court matter, heard in Los Angeles Superior Court. *(Verified baseline; the June 10 ruling and both bellwether trials are handled by Los Angeles Superior Court Judge Carolyn Kuhl, a state-court judge, per the Social Media Victims Law Center release and AP coverage.)*

3. **[ESTABLISHED]** The proceeding contains roughly 1,600 individual cases (plaintiffs). *(Verified baseline; corroborated by web search summary — "about 1,600 plaintiffs.")*

4. **[OBSERVED]** The bellwether trials are being presided over by Judge Carolyn Kuhl of the Los Angeles Superior Court. *(Social Media Victims Law Center release; web search summary of the June 10 ruling. Not independently confirmed from a court docket.)*

### (b) K.G.M. — the first bellwether verdict and the 70/30 split

5. **[ESTABLISHED]** The first bellwether is *K.G.M. v. Meta Platforms, Inc. et al.* (with Google as a co-defendant), tried in Los Angeles Superior Court. *(Verified baseline; corroborated by AP, NPR, and the plaintiff-counsel release.)*

6. **[ESTABLISHED]** On March 25, 2026, the jury returned a $6 million verdict against the defendants. *(Verified baseline; corroborated by AP/Washington Times photo caption and multiple web search summaries.)*

7. **[ESTABLISHED]** The $6 million is made up of $3 million in compensatory damages plus $3 million in punitive damages. *(Verified baseline; corroborated by AP, CNBC summary, and the plaintiff-counsel release.)*

8. **[ESTABLISHED]** The award was allocated 70% to Meta and 30% to Google — meaning Meta is responsible for $4.2 million and Google for $1.8 million. This is why some outlets report only Meta's $4.2 million share. *(Verified baseline; corroborated by web search summary of the June 10 coverage: "70% of the blame to Meta... $4.2 million... Google... 30%... $1.8 million.")*

9. **[OBSERVED]** The plaintiff is a young woman identified by the initials K.G.M. and by the first name "Kaley"; she was reported as 20 years old at the time of the July 2026 coverage and said she became addicted to Instagram and YouTube as a child, worsening her mental-health struggles. *(AP via Washington Times; web search summaries. Age/first name carried by secondary coverage only.)*

10. **[OBSERVED]** The jury found the companies negligent in the *design* of their apps — that features such as infinite scroll, autoplay, and push notifications were built to maximize engagement, that the companies knew of the risks, and that they failed to adequately warn young users. *(NPR / AP / web search summaries; characterization of the jury's findings, not a quotation from the verdict form.)*

### (c) The post-trial motions denied (a trial-court ruling, NOT an appeal)

11. **[ESTABLISHED]** On June 10, 2026, the trial court denied the defendants' motion for a new trial and their motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict (JNOV). *(Verified baseline; corroborated by the CNBC headline, the Social Media Victims Law Center release, and multiple web search summaries.)*

12. **[ESTABLISHED]** This June 10 denial is a **trial-court** decision (the same court that held the trial declining to disturb its own verdict). It is NOT an appellate ruling and does NOT mean an appeals court has reviewed or approved anything. *(Verified baseline; procedural-posture discipline. The ruling was issued by Los Angeles Superior Court Judge Carolyn Kuhl per the plaintiff-counsel release.)*

13. **[OBSERVED]** In denying the motions, Judge Kuhl reportedly wrote that the trial evidence "was sufficient to support a finding that Instagram design features were a substantial factor" in causing the plaintiff's harms and that Meta "never provided adequate warnings regarding the harms posed to minor users." *(Social Media Victims Law Center release — a plaintiff-side party release quoting the ruling; treat wording as reported, not verified against the order itself.)*

### (d) The appeals filed — who and when

14. **[ESTABLISHED]** Both defendants have filed notices of appeal of the K.G.M. verdict. *(Verified baseline; corroborated by AP via Washington Times / U.S. News.)*

15. **[ESTABLISHED]** Meta filed its notice of appeal first, in early July 2026. *(Verified baseline. The appeals have only just been *filed*; briefing and argument still lie ahead. *(Verified baseline; consistent with AP noting the companies "are expected to provide their arguments related to the appeal in later court filings.")*

## Discrepancies vs the verified baseline

- **D-1 — TikTok settlement date.** Baseline says TikTok settled "early July" 2026. Sources (The Next Web, citing a Bloomberg report; and the Business & Human Rights Resource Centre summary) put the TikTok settlement on **June 30, 2026** (reported "Tuesday"). This is late June, not early July. Both are recorded here; the baseline value is NOT overwritten. Likely a rounding/paraphrase difference rather than a factual conflict, but flagged for the human check.

- **D-2 — "Florida teen" framing of the second bellwether (the flagged trap).** Some coverage of the TikTok settlement (e.g., NBC News's headline, The Next Web's URL slug) foregrounds that the second-bellwether plaintiff, R.K.C. / "Russell," is a **Florida teen**. This could be misread as a *separate Florida court case*. Per the sources here it is NOT: the second bellwether is the **California** JCCP 5255 test case, tried in Los Angeles Superior Court before Judge Carolyn Kuhl; the plaintiff simply *resides* in Florida. The baseline (Meta + Snap, July 27, California) is consistent with this. Recorded as a clarification, not a conflict.

- **D-3 — One secondary summary mislabeled the second bellwether as "federal MDL."** The Next Web page, as summarized by the fetch, described the second bellwether as a "federal multidistrict litigation (MDL) in Los Angeles." That characterization is **rejected here as unreliable**: the same summary names **Judge Carolyn Kuhl**, who is a Los Angeles *Superior Court* (state) judge — and the Business & Human Rights Resource Centre explicitly frames the matter as the "second **California state-level**" trial. The federal MDL (MDL 3047) is a distinct proceeding before a different judge and must not be merged in. No baseline value changed; this is logged so the error is not silently carried forward.
